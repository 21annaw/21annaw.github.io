What is a Choropleth Map?

For our first assignment, we were tasked with creating a static and interactive choropleth map displaying one of the UN's Sustainable Devlopment Goals. choropleth map is typically based on quantitative data, it visually displays variation between different geographic areas or locations. It is therefore most effective, when dealing with clearly defined political borders, such as datasets involving government policies. Political borders are clearly defined, and can be easily visually compared on a choropleth map to show differences between states or countries.

Choropleth maps naturally come with both positive and negative characteristics. First of all, they’re easy to read and interpret – people are used to choropleth maps and can understand them. They also provide easy and immediate insight into differences between countries or geographical regions. However, on the other hand, the differences displayed on choropleth maps can be misleading. Since they’re based on averages, displaying certain data can create a certain idea about an area that may not be entirely accurate. This is called the “modifiable areal unit problem”, since it generalizes areas into uniform color. It doesn’t account for population density or distribution, for example. Additionally, choropleth maps must have an equivalent proportion of colors throughout the map, since the data is divided into a specific amount of categories – in my case, only 5 – which doesn’t allow for small, detailed variations to be visible.

SDG 5: Achieve Gender Equality and Empower All Women and Girls

Target 5.5: Ensure women's full and effective participation and equal opportunities for leadership at all levels of decision-making.

I choose to create a choropleth map displaying SDG Target 5.5, “Ensure women’s full and effective participation and equal opportunities for leadership at all levels of decision-making.”, which falls within Goal 5, “Achieve gender equality and empower all women and girls.”. The indicator chosen was the percentage proportion of seats held by women in the National Parliament, more specifically, the UN metadata from 2024. I found the data on this page: https://unstats.un.org/sdgs/metadata/files/Metadata-05-05-01a.pdf. Since it is from a more recent year, there seems to be more data missing than from previous years.

The different colors indicate the different percentage ranges, thus indicating the proportion of women in parliament in various countries. Countries who have women holding over 50% of the seats in parliament have been outlined and labeled specifically: Mexico, Nicaragua, Cuba, Rwanda, and the UAE. I wanted to highlight the countries that have more than 50% of seats in parliament being held by women, since there are only five (as per the UN’s 2024 dataset), with my labels. Therefore, I highlighted those countries with a light purple border to draw attention to them.

This map is an excellent example of how choropleth maps can be used to communicate important data, particularly to policy makers, governmental bodies and the general public. It immediately indicates how only five countries have reached the aforementioned goal of gender equality in politics. It allows for an instant overview of what the issue is, the current progress within the goal, and consequently, which regions should receive the most attention when implementing solutions for this specific goal.

Reflection

Skills gained: QGIS Mapping, Uploading and Cleaning Data, Data Visualization, Coding Interactive Maps

Since this was our first assignment, and my first exposure to QGIS, geographical and spatial data and website coding, I definitely ran into some hurdles. Essentially the entire creation process included a lot of redoing and repetition of steps as I was trying to adapt to the new software and figure it out. I spent a very long time on the color scheme as well, and all the graphics. I really wanted to create a neutral color scheme. A few other color scheme ideas were green or orange based, but I felt those were too close to passing a positive or negative judgement. To be safe and entirely neutral, I went with a color scheme of blue and purple instead. The most valuable things I learned were certainly the uploading and linking of datasets, which formed the basis of this map, but also certainly other maps in the future. This assignment also allowed me to explore QGIS as a complete novice, and gain some basic understanding of the software.
